# Neighborhood Emergency Preparedness Program Scenario

This document is the human-readable governance view of the fixed conversation embedded in the standalone CLI. The runner sends each prompt below verbatim and in order without adapting later turns to the model's responses.

## Turn 1

> I want to create and maintain a living A-to-B plan for a community emergency-preparedness program called ReadyTogether for a mixed urban and regional council area. It should help households, volunteers, local organizations, and council teams prepare for heatwaves, storms, bushfires, extended power loss, and temporary isolation without depending on a new software product. We are planning the complete program from the current idea through a twelve-month pilot and wider public rollout, but we are not running it during this conversation. Start the plan for this work.

## Turn 2

> The people involved include residents with no prior preparedness experience, experienced community volunteers, older people, disabled people, culturally and linguistically diverse households, schools, local businesses, emergency services, and council staff. Add the important outcomes and participation needs for each group without assuming that every participant has internet access, transport, spare money, or confidence dealing with authorities.

## Turn 3

> Use a fixed operating baseline for the plan: council sponsorship, delivery through existing community organizations, printed and accessible digital materials, in-person workshops, neighborhood exercises, phone and radio communication options, and a twelve-month pilot in three different communities. No custom application is part of the initial program. Record these as established constraints rather than reopening the delivery model later.

## Turn 4

> The initial program has five major workstreams: local risk understanding, household planning, volunteer capability, neighborhood coordination, and exercises and improvement. Describe what each workstream must achieve, how participants move through the program, and which outcomes must be demonstrated before wider rollout. Include people who join late and communities that already have informal preparedness groups.

## Turn 5

> Add a complete accessibility and participation task covering plain language, translated materials, interpreters, Auslan, large print, screen-reader-ready documents, low-literacy formats, sensory needs, mobility access, trauma-aware facilitation, childcare, transport, remote participation, and culturally safe engagement. Include observable criteria showing that excluded groups can genuinely participate.

## Turn 6

> The program needs to represent communities, neighborhoods, partner organizations, venues, facilitators, volunteers, training status, household-plan participation, accessibility requests, local risks, shared resources, exercises, observations, actions, referrals, and consented contact information. Integrate the information-management work into the plan, including minimum collection, ownership, retention, correction, access, safe sharing, and deletion.

## Turn 7

> Add governance and delivery work for council oversight, community partner agreements, emergency-service advice, facilitator authority, volunteer screening where appropriate, financial control, safeguarding, complaints, incident reporting, escalation, and decisions that must remain with qualified emergency or public-health authorities rather than volunteers.

## Turn 8

> Map the important participant journeys into the plan: hearing about the program, joining safely, identifying household needs, making a realistic plan, preparing supplies within budget, learning local warnings, connecting with neighbors, attending an exercise, receiving support after a gap is found, and updating the plan after a real event. Make the task order support proving these journeys during the pilot.

## Turn 9

> Communication must still work during outages and disrupted transport. Add realistic requirements for printed contact trees, local radio, phone and SMS, physical notice points, door knocking, battery and charging constraints, message confirmation, rumor control, language access, failed-contact escalation, and explicit boundaries preventing volunteers from representing unverified information as official direction.

## Turn 10

> Add safety, privacy, and safeguarding work. Cover vulnerable-person information, consent, family violence and address safety, child-safe participation, volunteer boundaries, medical and disability information, secure contact lists, photography, media consent, records access, retention, referrals, distress during exercises, real-incident interruption, and response to a participant or volunteer complaint.

## Turn 11

> Write a self-contained prompt that I can give to a research agent to investigate authoritative Australian emergency-preparedness guidance suitable for ReadyTogether. The researcher should compare all-hazards household planning, heatwave, storm, bushfire, power-outage and isolation guidance; warning systems; accessibility; culturally safe communication; volunteer boundaries; source authority; update cadence; licensing; and differences between national, state, council, and emergency-service responsibilities. Add the research and its decision point to the living plan, but do not perform the research now.

## Turn 12

> Add the work for converting approved authoritative guidance into local program materials. Include source review, jurisdiction and hazard mapping, plain-language adaptation, translations, accessibility review, attribution, version control, scheduled updates, urgent withdrawal, expert approval, facilitator briefings, and a visible distinction between general preparedness education and instructions issued during an actual emergency.

## Turn 13

> Build out volunteer recruitment, onboarding, training, and support. Cover role descriptions, suitability, checks where justified, boundaries, inclusive recruitment, practical training, observation, authorization, refresher learning, supervision, fatigue, reimbursement, recognition, grievances, removal from a role, and continuity when key volunteers leave.

## Turn 14

> The household-planning activity must be practical rather than aspirational. Add work for household contacts, medicines and assistive equipment, pets, transport, power needs, evacuation and shelter choices, important documents, affordable supplies, neighbor support, warning interpretation, rehearsals, seasonal review, and referrals when a household cannot resolve a critical gap alone.

## Turn 15

> Add a neighborhood exercise framework. Include safe scenario design, objectives, participant briefing, accessibility adjustments, observer roles, injects, communications loss, welfare checks, real-emergency cancellation, psychological safety, immediate debrief, evidence capture, corrective actions, ownership, deadlines, and follow-up proof that important weaknesses were addressed.

## Turn 16

> Add focused participation work for people who may be disproportionately affected during emergencies, including older residents, disabled people, people using powered medical equipment, socially isolated residents, renters, people without cars, newly arrived communities, people experiencing homelessness, and households under financial pressure. Avoid creating informal registries or promises that the program cannot safely maintain.

## Turn 17

> Define operational workflows for coordinators during normal delivery and when a real incident interrupts the program. Cover venue changes, facilitator absence, unsafe conditions, participant distress, incorrect materials, privacy incidents, volunteer misconduct, complaints, media inquiries, authority handoff, service referral, program suspension, and evidence needed to resume safely.

## Turn 18

> Write a detailed prompt for a research agent to study why households and neighborhoods do or do not sustain preparedness behavior. The research should examine trust, cost, competing priorities, risk perception, previous emergencies, cultural factors, disability access, volunteer fatigue, social connection, workshop attendance, practice and repetition, and reasons preparedness programs fade after initial enthusiasm. Add this research to the plan as a pilot-design input without conducting it now.

## Turn 19

> Add a proportionate measurement approach. The plan should show participation, accessibility, household-plan completion, warning knowledge, volunteer readiness, exercise learning, corrective-action closure, partner capability, and sustained engagement without collecting invasive personal data or claiming that attendance proves safety. Include qualitative feedback, data quality, ownership, retention, and honest interpretation.

## Turn 20

> Build out the program quality and evaluation strategy as a dependency-ordered part of the plan. Cover content review, facilitator observation, accessibility checks, safeguarding, pilot fidelity, exercise safety, communications testing, participant feedback, partner debriefs, corrective actions, independent review where appropriate, and evidence required before repeating or expanding the program.

## Turn 21

> Add the rollout path from co-design through material trials, facilitator rehearsal, three-community pilot, mid-pilot correction, final exercises, evaluation, council decision, wider partner onboarding, staged regional rollout, public communication, support readiness, and annual review. Include evidence gates and conditions that should pause, narrow, or stop expansion.

## Turn 22

> Add funding and sustainability work without assuming permanent council growth funding. Include facilitator time, venue access, translation, accessibility services, printing, insurance, volunteer reimbursement, equipment, partner grants, sponsorship boundaries, procurement, recurring refresh costs, succession, program ownership, and how communities retain useful capability if formal funding ends.

## Turn 23

> Write a self-contained research prompt comparing communication channels and accessible warning-support methods that could be taught through ReadyTogether. It should investigate official alert systems, local radio, SMS, phone trees, printed resources, community language channels, disability access, power and network failure, geographic coverage, misinformation risk, privacy, cost, maintenance, and the boundary between reinforcing official warnings and creating unofficial alerts. Add the resulting decision to the plan without selecting a channel.

## Turn 24

> Review the entire living plan now. Reconcile duplicated ideas, keep established constraints in the starting position, ensure the desired position describes a completed and sustainable program, correct the dependency order, and strengthen completion criteria for accessibility, safety, authority, privacy, volunteer capability, exercises, learning, operational readiness, and wider rollout. Keep unresolved delivery choices visible without turning them into user questions.

## Turn 25

> Pause the planning update long enough to create a reusable agent skill called public-safety-message-quality inside the scenario workspace. It should guide agents that draft preparedness education and exercise communications so messages are clear, accessible, source-conscious, jurisdiction-aware, explicit about uncertainty, suitable for translation, and careful not to impersonate official emergency warnings or give unsafe individualized direction. Create the complete skill now with only the files it genuinely needs, then record its use in the living plan.

## Turn 26

> Integrate the new public-safety-message-quality skill into the program plan. Identify which material drafting, translation briefing, facilitator communication, exercise, correction, partner review, testing, and release activities should use it, what evidence would show it was applied effectively, and how the program preserves qualified authority and community review.

## Turn 27

> Add a substantial communications-governance task covering authoritative sources, approvals, jurisdiction, accessible formats, translations, version history, seasonal review, corrections, rumor response, urgent withdrawal, partner distribution, facilitator scripts, participant reports, and communication when previously distributed advice changes. Reconcile this with the guidance-integration work and the public-safety-message-quality skill rather than creating parallel owners.

## Turn 28

> Review the public-safety-message-quality skill against the need we just established. Improve it if necessary so its description identifies the correct use boundary and its instructions remain general enough for household guides, workshop materials, exercise notices, volunteer scripts, translated briefs, corrections, and post-event explanations. Do not tailor it to the wording of this scenario; keep the skill reusable.

## Turn 29

> Perform a final planning pass before the structural change I will give you next. Check that every completion criterion is created by at least one task, every broad task has a meaningful outcome, research decisions occur before dependent program choices, rollout depends on adequate pilot and safety proof, and no important requirement has been reduced to a vague aspiration.

## Turn 30

> Change the program structure and revise the whole plan coherently. Instead of five centrally delivered workstreams, ReadyTogether should operate through neighborhood preparedness cells supported by one council coordination function, with each cell integrating household planning, volunteer capability, local communication, exercises, and improvement around its community. Establish clear ownership for authoritative guidance, local adaptation, participant safety, volunteer decisions, private information, shared resources, exercise actions, escalation, and regional assurance. Reorganize the broad tasks around this model without leaving the former centralized workstreams as competing owners, and reconcile every affected completion criterion.
