# Production Workflow and Risk Controls for the Public Demo

## Assignment

**Goal:** Compare concrete production workflows for a polished generic public 1080p YouTube demonstration below three minutes, with audio, project screen footage, brief intro/outro, occasional transitions, and an explicit explanation of the project plus Codex and GPT-5.6 use; provide an evidence-backed sequence and controls for deadline-sensitive delivery.

**Scope:** The constrained local discovery assignments 01–05, local tool availability already probed there, the official OpenAI Build Week rules, and primary Apple, YouTube, and vendor documentation needed to cross-check capture, delivery, account, and public-visibility risks. Comparison covers scriptable-first, native-free GUI, browser single-tool, and paid recorder-first workflows.

**Exclusions:** Hands-on production trials, paid acquisition, software installation, recording or publishing a video, a full privacy/security audit of third-party vendors, recommending a single overall winner, and the final cross-assignment synthesis.

## Findings

### Delivery Requirements Establish the Quality Gates

The rules require a clear public YouTube demo with audio that explains both what was built and how Codex and GPT-5.6 were used. They say the video should be below three minutes and judges need not watch after that point. The workflow therefore needs a complete narrative before a 2:50 target, then a final public-visibility and playback check rather than an upload-only check. [Official Rules](https://openai.devpost.com/rules) [Official overview](https://openai.devpost.com/)

**Evidence:** Assignment 02 records the governing video, audio, public-YouTube, English-accessibility, and intellectual-property requirements. YouTube says a public video is viewable by anyone, posted on the channel, and can appear in search; unlisted is a materially different visibility state. [YouTube visibility documentation](https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/157177?hl=en)

**Implication:** Use a hard content budget of 2:45–2:50: 0:00–0:10 problem/project framing, 0:10–0:35 product orientation, 0:35–1:50 real working demo, 1:50–2:25 how Codex and GPT-5.6 contributed, and 2:25–2:50 outcome plus call to action. This is a production plan, not a rule-mandated structure. Keep the substantive demo and the named technology explanation in the first two minutes so minor timing trims cannot remove required content.

### A Staged Sequence Keeps Creative Work Separate From Irreversible Gates

1. **Lock the story before capture.** Ask Codex for a 2:45–2:50 narration, a shot list tied to real project states, onscreen wording that explicitly names Codex and GPT-5.6, and a rights/provenance ledger for every non-original media item. Read the full script aloud once and remove unprovable claims.
2. **Prepare a clean capture state.** Use generic/public data, hide notifications, close unrelated applications, remove credentials and personal data, set the target window/region to 16:9, and test microphone levels. Apple supports selected-area recording, a mic choice, and click markers; use those only when they clarify an interaction. [Apple screen recording](https://support.apple.com/en-us/102618)
3. **Run the selected workflow’s proof gate.** Capture 10–15 seconds, add the actual title/audio/asset classes intended for the final, and export 1080p. Check account state, watermark absence, selected asset entitlement, audible narration, and local playback before recording the full performance.
4. **Capture modularly.** Record one clean establishing segment, two or three feature actions, and a separate narrated Codex/GPT-5.6 explanation. Keep retakes as separate clips; this limits one mistake to one segment and enables straightforward trim/replace work.
5. **Assemble only essential polish.** Add one short branded/original intro, readable identifiers, two or fewer transition styles, restrained background audio if it is cleared, and a short outro. Prioritize the live product view and intelligible audio over decorative animation.
6. **Render and inspect locally.** Check duration, dimensions, frame rate, audio stream, and repeated playback points: start, every transition, the Codex/GPT-5.6 explanation, and final frame. For the scriptable route, `ffprobe` makes stream metadata repeatable; inspect the rendered image/video playback separately for clipped text, cursor distraction, and audible defects.
7. **Publish early enough to recover.** Upload the checked MP4 to YouTube, wait for the intended quality rendition, set visibility to Public, play the published link while signed out or in a separate profile, and copy the exact URL into the submission. YouTube recommends progressive H.264 MP4 with AAC-LC/Opus, matching capture frame rate, and 1080p SDR bitrates of 8 Mbps at 24–30 fps or 12 Mbps at 48–60 fps. [YouTube encoding guidance](https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/1722171?hl=en)

**Evidence:** The sequence applies documented capture controls, the locally present FFmpeg/ffprobe path, competition’s audio/public/demo constraints, and YouTube’s delivery guidance. It also observes the discovery reports’ repeated caveat that watermark, plan, account, capture-permission, and browser-control claims require a live proof on the exact route before production.

**Implication:** A proof-export gate and modular footage are the highest-leverage controls: they expose entitlement and technical blockers before the full edit, while making a workflow switch possible without redoing the project demonstration.

### Risk Controls Prioritize Submission Validity Before Visual Ambition

| Risk | Trigger or consequence | Control and fallback |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Required narrative omitted or too late** | The video lacks audible explanation of the project, Codex, or GPT-5.6; judges may stop at three minutes. | Put a spoken, on-screen named explanation in the first 2:25 and use a 2:50 timing ceiling. Fallback: trim decorative intro/outro first, not demo or explanation. |
| **Wrong YouTube visibility** | A private/unlisted link conflicts with the rule’s public-video requirement. | Set Public deliberately, then open the published link in a signed-out/separate profile and retain the URL. |
| **Watermark, paid asset, or export entitlement surprise** | The final is visually unsuitable or its media rights are unclear. | Proof-export with the exact plan and asset classes; retain an asset ledger. Fallback: original title cards, original narration, local FFmpeg finish, and no stock music. |
| **Sensitive material appears in capture or hosted editor** | Credentials, personal data, internal messages, or unreleased information is exposed or uploaded. | Use generic/public data; inspect every frame before upload; prefer local capture/render for sensitive footage. Fallback: recapture only the affected modular segment and remove the hosted editor from the chain. |
| **Audio failure** | Audio is missing, muffled, clipped, or obscured; the submission fails a stated requirement. | Record a 10–15 second mic test, monitor with headphones, and review the encoded export plus YouTube rendition. Fallback: replace narration track in the local/scriptable or GUI editor without recapturing screen footage. |
| **Tool/account/network failure** | A hosted editor, subscription, sign-in, or upload process blocks completion. | Complete the proof gate before full assembly and export a local master before upload. Fallback: Screenshot plus FFmpeg, which is already locally available, for capture/stitching. |
| **Time lost to complex polish** | Multiple revisions consume the deadline without improving evaluation-relevant clarity. | Freeze one title treatment and no more than two transition styles after the proof export. Fallback: a clean single-cut demo with original title cards and narration is preferable to an unfinished complex timeline. |
| **Publish or processing delay** | The submission link is unavailable or only low-quality processing is complete near deadline. | Upload the final checked master before the submission window’s last hours and verify the public link/1080p rendition. Fallback: preserve a tested local master for re-upload only if time remains. |

**Evidence:** The first two controls follow the authoritative Devpost requirements and YouTube’s documented visibility meanings. Entitlement, hosted-media, and watermark controls respond to the official vendor terms summarized in assignments 04–05. The local fallback is supported by assignment 01’s installed-tool probe and the documented macOS Screenshot controls. [Official Rules](https://openai.devpost.com/rules) [YouTube visibility documentation](https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/157177?hl=en)

**Implication:** Treat publication, rights, audio, and visibility as release-blocking checks. Visual elaboration remains discretionary after those controls pass.

### Codex Work Should Be Durable and Auditable

Codex has the strongest role where it creates reusable production artifacts: a fact-checked narrative, time budget, shot list, title/overlay copy, source/rights ledger, capture checklist, local render commands, and `ffprobe` inspection command. Browser or Computer Use can assist supervised GUI steps, but the discovered local capabilities do not make a hosted or desktop editor a dependable unattended production pipeline.

**Evidence:** Assignment 01 distinguishes Codex Browser/Computer Use UI operation from media composition and finds FFmpeg/ffprobe available. Assignment 03 identifies repository-held specifications, render commands, and output inspection as the durable scriptable boundary. Assignments 04–05 identify hosted and GUI workflows as interactive/account-dependent.

**Implication:** Preserve Codex-generated script/specification materials alongside the submission evidence. This makes the required explanation credible, makes revisions traceable, and avoids representing UI automation as a video-editor capability it does not provide.
