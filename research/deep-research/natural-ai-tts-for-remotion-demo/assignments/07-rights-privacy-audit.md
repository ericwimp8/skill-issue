# TTS Rights, Consent, Privacy, and Publication Audit

## Assignment

**Goal:** Identify the lowest-risk text-to-speech publication paths for a finalist's public prize-hackathon YouTube demo, focusing on output rights, commercial and promotional use, attribution, consent, retention, privacy, watermarking, and service eligibility.

**Scope:** Internet-only review of current official rules, terms, privacy notices, documentation, help articles, repositories, licenses, and model cards for ElevenLabs, OpenAI API speech, Speechify Studio/API, Deepgram Aura, Google Cloud Text-to-Speech, Murf, Descript, Kokoro through MLX-Audio, and Chatterbox. “Commercial” is treated conservatively to include a public submission competing for prizes and available for sponsor promotion. This is product-risk research, not legal advice.

**Exclusions:** Voice naturalness, pronunciation quality, latency, pricing comparisons beyond rights-relevant free/trial distinctions, and legal conclusions about whether machine-generated audio is copyrightable in any jurisdiction.

## Findings

### Finding 1: The contest's ownership warranty makes explicit output ownership materially safer than a mere use license

The rules require a public YouTube video, prohibit unauthorized third-party material, require authorized use of third-party integrations, and require the submission to be original and solely owned without another person or entity holding a right or interest. They also authorize Sponsor and Devpost to promote the submission and use contributor identity attributes for three years. A vendor's “commercial use” permission therefore answers only part of the publication question: a finalist also needs a defensible basis for the ownership warranty and promotional reuse.

**Evidence:** The [official rules](https://openai.devpost.com/rules) require an under-three-minute publicly visible YouTube video with clear audio; require permission for third-party trademarks and copyrighted material; permit open-source components only when their licenses are followed; require original, solely owned submissions; and grant Sponsor and Devpost promotional rights. The vendor terms below split into explicit user ownership, perpetual commercial permission, or a narrower output-use license.

**Implication:** Prefer built-in or vendor-owned stock voices under terms that expressly assign or recognize output ownership. Avoid community, actor-derived, or cloned voices unless the voice owner has granted written rights broad enough for the submission, public YouTube publication, prize promotion, and the rules' three-year promotional use. For any service that grants only commercial use, obtain written vendor and organizer confirmation before relying on it for the contest ownership warranty.

### Finding 2: OpenAI API speech with a built-in voice is the clearest low-risk path reviewed

OpenAI's API contract expressly places output ownership with the customer as between the parties, while the API speech guide requires disclosure that the voice is AI-generated. Built-in API voices avoid an underlying human voice contributor. Custom voices introduce consent recordings, eligibility restrictions, and a supplemental agreement. ChatGPT Voice is governed separately and carries a non-commercial distribution restriction, so the downloadable speech used in the submission should be created through the API speech endpoint.

**Evidence:** Section 4.1 of the [Services Agreement](https://openai.com/policies/services-agreement/) says the customer owns output and OpenAI assigns any output rights it has; section 4.2 says API customer content is not used to improve services unless the customer explicitly agrees. The [speech guide](https://developers.openai.com/api/docs/guides/text-to-speech) requires a clear AI-voice disclosure and documents built-in voices; custom voices are limited to eligible customers and require consent and sample recordings plus supplemental terms. The [Service Terms](https://openai.com/policies/service-terms/) limit ChatGPT Voice Output to non-commercial use and prohibit distributing or repackaging it as standalone audio. [API data controls](https://platform.openai.com/docs/models/default-usage-policies-by-endpoint) say API data is not used for training by default, while abuse-monitoring logs may retain content for up to 30 days; Zero Data Retention and Modified Abuse Monitoring require approval. On termination, the Services Agreement provides for deletion of customer content within 30 days subject to legal exceptions.

**Implication:** Generate with an ordinary built-in API voice, include “AI-generated voice created with the OpenAI API” in video credits and the YouTube description, and archive the rendered audio. Do not export or repurpose ChatGPT Voice. Treat custom voice access as an eligibility blocker until the account shows access and the supplemental agreement is accepted. No reviewed API speech document identified an audible or imperceptible watermark requirement, so disclosure must remain explicit rather than inferred from provenance technology.

### Finding 3: ElevenLabs is publishable on a paid plan, but voice source and generation date control the risk

ElevenLabs expressly distinguishes free output from paid output. Free and unsigned use is non-commercial and attribution-bound; upgrading later does not cure the status of audio generated while free. Paid, non-beta output receives commercial rights that survive cancellation. The user's output rights are strong as between the parties, but ElevenLabs retains broad licenses to content and custom voice models, and Voice Library user voices can leave a human contributor with interests in the underlying model.

**Evidence:** The [Terms of Use](https://elevenlabs.io/terms-of-use) limit free users to non-commercial use, allow paid users commercial use, say users retain their output rights as between user and ElevenLabs, and grant ElevenLabs broad, perpetual, irrevocable content and custom-voice-model licenses. The [publishing guidance](https://help.elevenlabs.io/hc/en-us/articles/13313564601361-Can-I-publish-the-content-I-generate-on-the-platform) requires `elevenlabs.io` or `11.ai` attribution for publicly shared free output, excludes free and Beta output from commercial use, and says paid-era commercial rights persist indefinitely. The [post-subscription article](https://help.elevenlabs.io/hc/en-us/articles/15993008593297-What-happens-to-my-content-after-my-subscription-ends) confirms surviving commercial rights but does not promise continued cloud access. The [Voice Library Addendum](https://elevenlabs.io/vla) recognizes user-supplied voice models and preserves already generated outputs after a model is removed. Data may be used to improve models unless the account opts out for new data; ordinary TTS stores inputs and outputs by default, while [zero retention](https://elevenlabs.io/docs/eleven-api/resources/zero-retention-mode) is limited to selected Enterprise accounts and excludes cloning samples. The prohibited-use and cloning documents place responsibility for authorization and deceptive impersonation on the user; voice verification is a safeguard rather than proof of consent.

**Implication:** Use a paid plan, a non-beta ElevenLabs-owned stock voice or Voice Design voice, and generate only after the paid entitlement begins. Avoid Voice Library user clones for the contest unless the voice contributor's rights and the organizer's ownership interpretation are confirmed. Opt out of model improvement before uploading sensitive material, archive audio before cancellation, and disclose AI narration even when attribution is not contractually required. ElevenLabs says audio is traceable to an account, but the reviewed materials do not establish an audible watermark; traceability is neither disclosure nor proof of ownership.

### Finding 4: SpeechifyAI Free API permits commercial use, while Studio Free remains publication-ineligible

SpeechifyAI API and Speechify Studio are separate plan surfaces with materially different free-tier rights. The current SpeechifyAI API pricing page expressly says its Free API plan costs $0, requires no credit card, includes 50,000 TTS characters per month, and permits commercial use. Speechify Studio Free separately lacks commercial rights and downloadable MP3 output; paid Studio advertises perpetual ownership of the exported audio and commercial rights. The API supplemental terms apply to the API across plan tiers, classify Synthetic Output as both “Output” and “Customer Content,” and require commercially reasonable AI disclosure. They do not provide different output-ownership language for Free versus paid API plans or expressly assign generated-output ownership to the customer. Paying for API capacity therefore does not cure or strengthen the API ownership wording; it changes allowance and features.

**Evidence:** The current [SpeechifyAI API pricing page](https://speechify.ai/pricing) states “Build and ship on both APIs,” “No credit card,” 50,000 monthly TTS characters, and “Commercial use” for Free. The [AI Voice API supplemental terms](https://speechify.com/terms-ai-voice-api/) say they and the linked general/business terms form the entire API agreement, control conflicts, and classify Synthetic Output as Output and Customer Content; the same terms require AI disclosure and written consent for cloned voices. The current [general/business terms](https://speechify.com/terms/) do not claim ownership of user material, but their terminology is not aligned cleanly with the API supplement's Customer Content/Output definitions and they do not contain an express assignment of API output rights. The [Studio pricing page](https://speechify.com/pricing-studio/) separately says Studio Free lacks commercial usage rights and MP3 download, while paid Studio users own audio output and commercial rights in perpetuity. The [Studio terms](https://speechify.com/studio-terms/) govern that browser product and its cloning eligibility, not SpeechifyAI Free API publication rights. The [privacy policy](https://speechify.com/privacy/) permits use of user content for research and service improvement, places processing in the United States, and permits retention after deletion for legitimate or legal purposes.

**Implication:** SpeechifyAI Free API with a vendor-provided stock voice is a documented commercial route for the prize submission; retain dated captures of the Free API pricing and API terms, disclose AI narration, and archive the output. Do not substitute a Speechify Studio Free preview or export. Upgrade the API only for capacity or features, not for ownership rights. For the Build Week “solely owned” warranty, Free and paid SpeechifyAI API have the same caveat: commercial permission and Customer Content classification are supportive but are not an express assignment. If the organizer requires express assignment language, obtain confirmation or use the OpenAI API built-in-voice route. Avoid Community Voices and cloning unless eligibility, consent, and promotional rights are documented.

### Finding 5: Descript paid stock speakers are comparatively low risk, but its third-party model and privacy layers should be recorded

Descript treats input and output as User Content and recognizes user ownership of output to the extent protectable. Stock and custom AI Speakers are permitted commercially, but custom speakers require recorded consent. Current Descript text-to-speech documentation identifies ElevenLabs models as the generation layer, and free video exports carry a visible Descript watermark. Descript can use project content to improve products unless the user opts out, while custom-speaker training data and voiceprints may have longer retention.

**Evidence:** The [Terms](https://www.descript.com/terms) say the user owns output as between user and Descript to the extent protectable, license User Content to Descript for service operation and improvement, and warn that generative tools can rely on third parties; Descript says its third-party contracts bar those providers from training on user content. [AI Speaker guidance](https://help.descript.com/hc/en-us/articles/10119641262221-Create-a-new-AI-Speaker) requires the speaker's explicit recorded authorization. The [text-to-speech article](https://help.descript.com/hc/en-us/articles/10166020211085-Generate-text-to-speech-audio) identifies current ElevenLabs Multilingual models. The [export article](https://help.descript.com/hc/en-us/articles/10255814959245-Export-an-mp4-video-or-a-GIF) says free video exports have a visible Descript watermark and paid exports remove it. The [privacy policy](https://www.descript.com/privacy) allows product improvement unless the user disables sharing, permits controlled human access, and describes retention of de-associated custom-speaker data and voiceprints for stated purposes.

**Implication:** Prefer a paid stock AI Speaker, disable project-data sharing before upload, disclose AI narration, and render/archive audio separately from the final Remotion video. Do not treat the free visual brand watermark as AI provenance. Because the current stock TTS layer is a third-party model, retain the applicable Descript terms and request confirmation if the organizer interprets “solely owned” more strictly than Descript's “to the extent protectable” allocation.

### Finding 6: Deepgram Aura, Google Cloud TTS, and Murf allow useful publication paths but leave output ownership less explicit

These services can produce stock-voice narration without cloning, but their reviewed official terms do not give the same direct user-output ownership statement as OpenAI API, paid Speechify Studio, or Descript. Deepgram grants royalty-free lawful use of output while reserving service IP; Google strongly limits TTS content logging but the general customer-data clauses do not clearly identify classic Text-to-Speech output as customer-owned; Murf grants paid commercial rights and post-cancellation business usage rights without an equally clear generated-output ownership clause.

**Evidence:** Deepgram's [Business Terms](https://deepgram.com/business/Business_TOS.pdf) permit royalty-free lawful output use, prohibit representing output as human-generated, reserve Deepgram service IP, require consents for identifiable people, permit de-identified output use, and allow Deepgram to demand cessation or deletion when third-party rights are implicated. Its [Model Improvement Partnership](https://developers.deepgram.com/docs/the-deepgram-model-improvement-partnership-program) is voluntary and supports per-request `mip_opt_out=true`. Google classifies Text-to-Speech as a Pre-Trained API in its [services summary](https://cloud.google.com/terms/services); [TTS data logging](https://docs.cloud.google.com/text-to-speech/docs/data-logging) says the service is stateless and does not log submitted text or returned audio. The [Cloud Terms](https://cloud.google.com/terms/) preserve customer IP in Customer Data, but the reviewed classic TTS terms do not clearly allocate generated-audio ownership; Gemini-TTS is marked Pre-GA in the [product documentation](https://docs.cloud.google.com/text-to-speech/docs/gemini-tts). Murf's [Terms](https://murf.ai/legal/terms-of-service) grant paid commercial use of Murf-created voices, disclaim storage obligations, and require written cloning consent, while its help center says paid commercial rights survive subscription end. The free trial cannot export audio. Murf's privacy policy describes deletion after termination, while other official security guidance has used a different deletion window, making retention timing unreliable without current account confirmation.

**Implication:** Treat all three as confirmation-required for the contest's “solely owned” warranty. If chosen, use a generally available paid stock voice, avoid previews and custom cloning, disclose AI narration, opt out of Deepgram model improvement, and archive the generated file. Google classic TTS offers the strongest reviewed privacy posture of this group; Deepgram has an express non-human disclosure duty; Murf requires a paid export. No reviewed official source established an audible or imperceptible output watermark for these three.

### Finding 7: Local open models minimize vendor retention but permissive code/model licenses do not resolve every output-rights question

Kokoro through MLX-Audio and Chatterbox can run locally, keeping the script and reference audio off vendor services. Their Apache-2.0 and MIT licenses authorize broad software/model use, but neither license by itself proves entrant ownership of every generated waveform or clears all training and voice-source interests. Kokoro's model card provides only partial provenance. Chatterbox adds an imperceptible PerTh watermark and explicitly supports voice cloning, making consent evidence essential.

**Evidence:** The [Kokoro model card](https://huggingface.co/hexgrad/Kokoro-82M) uses Apache-2.0, welcomes production deployment, and describes a mixture of public-domain, permissively licensed, attributed, and synthetic training sources; the [voice notes](https://huggingface.co/hexgrad/Kokoro-82M/blob/main/VOICES.md) identify some source attributions but do not provide a complete per-voice rights chain. [MLX-Audio](https://github.com/Blaizzy/mlx-audio) is MIT-licensed, which governs the wrapper rather than replacing the model's license. Chatterbox's [repository license](https://github.com/resemble-ai/chatterbox/blob/master/LICENSE) and [model card](https://huggingface.co/ResembleAI/chatterbox) are MIT, while the [official model page](https://www.resemble.ai/learn/models/chatterbox) instructs users to clone only voices they may use. Chatterbox includes a PerTh imperceptible neural watermark designed to survive compression and editing. The [watermarking release note](https://www.resemble.ai/resources/chatterbox-multilingual-v3-tts-with-embedded-watermarking-for-25-languages) describes it as a generated-by-Chatterbox signal, not proof of the user's ownership or the reference speaker's consent.

**Implication:** For privacy-sensitive scripts, local execution is preferable because there is no service account, remote retention, or cancellation dependency. Preserve Apache/MIT notices when redistributing software or model files; credit the model and wrapper in project documentation; preserve any source-specific attribution attached to the selected Kokoro voice. Use only a self-recorded or fully released Chatterbox reference, keep the PerTh watermark intact, and add an explicit human-readable AI disclosure. Obtain organizer confirmation before relying on either local model for the contest's ownership warranty because model licensing and output ownership are distinct questions.

### Finding 8: A conservative publication record is as important as the selected vendor

Rights can depend on the plan, generation date, voice source, model status, and whether the entrant retained an export. A reproducible evidence packet reduces ambiguity caused by cancellation, later term changes, or a voice disappearing from a library.

**Evidence:** ElevenLabs expressly ties commercial rights to the plan active when output was generated and preserves paid-era rights after cancellation; Speechify's current pricing differs from broader language in Studio terms; Murf's retention statements have differed across official pages; Descript's TTS provider is documented as changeable; Voice Library models can be removed while prior outputs survive. OpenAI, Deepgram, and Speechify impose explicit disclosure duties in at least some applicable TTS or cloning flows.

**Implication:** Before publication:

1. Generate on a qualifying commercial entitlement and save the account tier, generation date, voice/model identifier, usage record, dated terms pages, and invoice when payment is required. SpeechifyAI Free API is a qualifying documented commercial entitlement; Speechify Studio Free is not.
2. Choose a vendor-owned built-in or stock voice. Avoid celebrity, community, marketplace, deceased-person, and third-party cloned voices.
3. If cloning is unavoidable, retain a signed release covering cloning, public YouTube use, prize competition, Sponsor/Devpost promotion for three years, cross-border processing, and the right to retain the final synthetic recording.
4. Put a plain disclosure in both the video credits and description: “Narration uses an AI-generated voice created with [vendor/model]; no human speaker is being represented.”
5. Archive the final WAV/MP3 and project evidence before downgrade, cancellation, voice removal, or account deletion.
6. Keep imperceptible provenance watermarks intact; identify visible free-tier brand watermarks separately; never substitute either for disclosure or consent.
7. Keep scripts free of unnecessary personal or sensitive data, enable available training opt-outs before generation, and prefer local execution or approved zero-retention modes when confidentiality matters.
8. Avoid showing vendor logos or other third-party marks in the demo unless permission is documented, as the contest rules separately restrict third-party trademarks.

### Finding 9: The lowest-risk publication paths are stock-voice paths with explicit output ownership

The strongest paths combine explicit user output ownership, commercial/public permission, no human clone, an ordinary generally available product surface, local possession of the result, and visible disclosure.

**Evidence:** OpenAI API, paid Speechify Studio, and Descript each provide comparatively direct output-ownership language. SpeechifyAI Free and paid API plans share the same API supplemental terms: Free is expressly commercial and Synthetic Output is Customer Content, but no reviewed API term expressly assigns output rights or gives paid API stronger ownership language. ElevenLabs recognizes user output rights and durable paid commercial use but adds plan-date, Beta, vendor-license, and Voice Library complexity. Deepgram and Murf focus on output-use or commercial rights rather than explicit ownership; Google's classic TTS allocation is incomplete in the reviewed terms; permissive local model licenses do not alone allocate generated-audio ownership.

**Implication:** Recommended rights-first order:

1. **OpenAI API built-in voice:** clearest contract allocation; mandatory AI disclosure; ordinary API availability must be confirmed for the entrant's supported country and account.
2. **Paid Speechify Studio stock voice:** explicit perpetual output ownership and commercial rights; use the paid tier and retain dated proof because free-tier documents conflict.
3. **Paid Descript stock AI Speaker:** output ownership to the extent protectable; paid export avoids the visible Descript video watermark; retain the current third-party model disclosure.
4. **Paid ElevenLabs-owned stock or Voice Design voice:** durable commercial rights and user output rights; exclude Beta output and Voice Library user clones.

**SpeechifyAI Free API remains commercially eligible and may be the best overall product route, but it sits below this express-ownership ranking because paying for the API does not strengthen its ownership wording.** Deepgram Aura, classic Google Cloud TTS, paid Murf, Chatterbox, and Kokoro/MLX-Audio remain viable only after resolving the contest-specific ownership blocker. A vendor being technically available or commercially licensed is not enough to satisfy the rules' warranty without that confirmation.

## Notes

- **True blocker — contest interpretation:** Ask Devpost/OpenAI whether “solely owned” accepts commercially permitted output classified as customer content when the vendor does not expressly assign generated-output ownership. This affects SpeechifyAI Free and paid API equally, and also affects Deepgram Aura, Google Cloud TTS, Murf, Kokoro/MLX-Audio, and Chatterbox.
- **True blocker — account eligibility:** Custom OpenAI voices and ElevenLabs zero-retention mode are account-gated; Speechify Studio cloning is geographically restricted; preview/Beta products may lack commercial production rights. Confirm the feature inside the entrant's account before recording.
- **Unsupported claim — no watermark:** Absence of a watermark statement in reviewed official documentation is not evidence that no vendor-side provenance signal exists. Only Chatterbox's imperceptible PerTh watermark and Descript's free-export visual brand watermark were affirmatively documented.
- **Caveat — output ownership:** Contractual ownership “as between the parties” does not establish that generated audio is independently copyrightable or free of every third-party claim.
- **Caveat — changing terms:** SpeechifyAI API Free currently permits commercial use, while Speechify Studio Free does not; keep those product surfaces distinct and preserve dated copies of controlling pricing, terms, and account screens. Murf deletion timing, Descript's generation provider, and model-card provenance can also change or conflict.
- **Caveat — open-model provenance:** Kokoro's training and voice-source statements are model-card claims with incomplete per-voice documentation. Chatterbox's watermark supports synthetic-origin detection but does not validate reference-speaker consent.
