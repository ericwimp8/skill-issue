## Material Limitations

- The supplied scan is retained as evidence of what one synthetic checker reported. It does not prove the reported user impact, priority, remediation, absence of other barriers, or WCAG conformance.
- No rendered page was available, so visual prominence, computed accessibility roles/names, focus behavior, zoom/reflow, forced-colors behavior, and status announcements remain unverified.
- The meaning, update frequency, and operational urgency of the service states were not supplied. Priority therefore uses the dashboard's apparent status-monitoring purpose and records that dependency.

## Prioritized Findings

### High — Service state is conveyed by color-only, semantically empty indicators

- **Evidence class:** Observed authored implementation; inferred end-user behavior pending rendering and assistive-technology confirmation.
- **Affected users and task impact:** People with color-vision differences, low vision, or limited/monochrome color displays may be unable to distinguish Search's green state from Payments' red state. Screen-reader and refreshable-braille users may receive no status at all because the status spans are empty and expose no authored text. This can seriously impede the dashboard's primary task: determining which service needs attention.
- **Evidence:** `index.html` lines 8–10 define identical circular indicators whose only authored difference is green versus red background. Lines 16–17 place empty status spans beside the service names. `scan-results.json` line 4 records that `synthetic-checker` reported `color-only-status` for `.status` with tool severity `minor`.
- **Reproduction or inspection steps:** Open the supplied HTML source; inspect `.status`, `.good`, and `.bad` at lines 8–10; then inspect the empty status spans at lines 16–17. Confirm that neither row contains status text, a non-color visual cue, nor authored semantic status content.
- **Authoritative guidance:** [WCAG 2.2 SC 1.4.1](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/#use-of-color) requires color not be the only visual means of conveying information. The [Understanding document](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/use-of-color.html) explains that text or shape can supplement color and that a visible alternative remains necessary even if assistive technology receives the information.
- **Priority rationale:** **High**, independently of the checker's `minor` label. The risk affects every status row and the central monitoring purpose; users who cannot distinguish the colors have no status text or other direct cue in this view. Opening a detail view might be a workaround, but whether those buttons expose the status is unverified. Confidence is high in the authored color-only pattern and moderate in operational criticality because the business consequences of a bad state are unknown.
- **Remediation direction:** At the status-data rendering owner, expose each state as concise visible text (for example, “Operational” and “Disrupted”) associated with its service. Retain color only as a redundant cue; an additional distinguishable shape/icon may help visually but must also carry equivalent authored text for nonvisual access. If states update dynamically, use an appropriate status-announcement pattern based on the update's urgency rather than adding ARIA to the decorative dot alone.
- **Human or assistive-technology follow-up:** In a supported browser, verify both states at default colors, grayscale, forced-colors/high-contrast mode, and with a color-vision simulation; the state must remain distinguishable without color. With VoiceOver + Safari and NVDA + Firefox, navigate each row and confirm the service name and current state are announced together. Trigger a real status change and confirm the update is announced once, at an urgency appropriate to the event, without moving focus.
- **Confidence and limitations:** High confidence that the supplied source uses color as the only authored state distinction. The exact computed accessibility tree, announcement behavior, detail-view workaround, and consequences of missing a state are unverified.

### Medium — The apparent page heading has no heading semantics

- **Evidence class:** Observed absence of heading markup; source-backed inference that `.page-title` is intended as the page heading.
- **Affected users and task impact:** Screen-reader and refreshable-braille users who navigate by headings may miss the page's primary orientation point or be unable to jump to it. This makes navigation and understanding more effortful, although the small supplied page remains available by linear reading.
- **Evidence:** `index.html` line 14 renders “Service overview” in a generic `div` with class `page-title`; the document contains no `h1`–`h6` element. `scan-results.json` line 5 records that `synthetic-checker` reported `heading-structure` for `.page-title` with tool severity `serious`.
- **Reproduction or inspection steps:** Inspect line 14 and search the supplied document for `h1` through `h6`. Confirm that the apparent title is a generic element and no native heading is authored.
- **Authoritative guidance:** [WCAG 2.2 SC 1.3.1](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/#info-and-relationships) requires structure conveyed through presentation to be programmatically determinable or available in text. Its [Understanding document](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/info-and-relationships.html) identifies headings as structural relationships and native `h1`–`h6` markup as a sufficient HTML technique. The criterion mapping depends on confirming that this text is presented as a heading.
- **Priority rationale:** **Medium**, independently of the checker's `serious` label. The missing semantic heading affects navigation and orientation for a repeated assistive-technology strategy, but this fixture has one short view, all content remains reachable in source order, and linear reading is a reasonable though less efficient workaround. Confidence is high that heading semantics are absent and moderate that the element is visually/intentionally a heading because no rendered style or product intent was tested.
- **Remediation direction:** At the page-structure owner, mark the page's actual top-level heading with the appropriate native HTML heading level, normally an `h1` when this is the page's principal heading. Choose the level from the complete rendered document hierarchy rather than from desired appearance; keep appearance in CSS.
- **Human or assistive-technology follow-up:** Render the complete dashboard shell and confirm visually that “Service overview” functions as the primary page heading. With VoiceOver + Safari and NVDA + Firefox, inspect the heading list and heading-key navigation; expect one descriptive top-level heading in a logical hierarchy and no skipped or duplicated level caused by the surrounding application shell.
- **Confidence and limitations:** The absence of heading markup is observed. The final level, complete hierarchy, actual visual treatment, and assistive-technology navigation result are unverified.

## Prioritized Next Actions

1. Add visible, programmatically available state text for every service; verify non-color perception and screen-reader output.
2. Replace the apparent title container with the correct native heading in the complete page hierarchy; verify heading navigation.
3. Render the dashboard and execute the focused keyboard, forced-colors, zoom/reflow, and dynamic-update checks listed above.
4. Run a broader human-led accessibility evaluation, including representative disabled users for the service-monitoring journey, before making conformance claims.

## Review Boundary

This first-pass review identifies evidence and risks within the stated scope. It does not establish overall accessibility, certification, or standards conformance. Automated results and limited manual checks cannot replace comprehensive evaluation and testing with disabled people and relevant assistive technologies.
